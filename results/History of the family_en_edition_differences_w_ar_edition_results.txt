 In biblical times, men sought to prove their descent from the family of the prophet Moses in order to be accepted into the priesthood.<ref name="Family Chronicle"/>

[[Ancient Rome|Roman]] families would include everyone within a household under the authoritarian role of the father, the [[pater familias]]; this included grown children and the slaves of the household.<ref>{{Cite web| last = | first = | title = The Illustrated History of the Roman Empire | accessdate = 2009-07-17| url =http://www.roman-empire.net/society/society.html }}</ref> Children born outside of marriage, from common and legal [[concubinage]], could not inherit the father's property or name; instead, they belong to the social group and family of their mothers'.<ref name="Letourneau 1904">Letourneau 1904.</ref>

Most ancient cultures like those of [[Assyria]], [[Ancient Egypt|Egypt,]] and [[China]], kept records of successors in the ruling [[dynasties]] to legitimize their power as [[Divinity|divine]] in origin.<ref name="Family Chronicle"/> Both the [[Inca]] king and the Egyptian [[Pharaoh]] claimed that they were direct descendants of the [[Sun God]].<ref name="Family Chronicle"/>  Many cultures, such as the Inca of South America, the [[Kinte]] of Africa, and the [[Māori people|Māori]] of New Zealand, did not have a written language and kept the history of their descent as an oral tradition.<ref name="Family Chronicle"/>

Many cultures used other symbols to document their history of descent.<ref name="Family Chronicle"/>  The [[totem poles]] are indigenous to the people of the [[Pacific Northwest]]
